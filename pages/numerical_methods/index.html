Introduction

While analytical solutions are available for solving simple DEs, they are rare for complex real-world problems. Numerical methods are techniques that enable us to tackle a vast spectrum of DEs, spanning from the elementary to the highly intricate. We will explore the principles of discretization, numerical integration, and iterative algorithms that form the backbone of these methods. With a focus on practicality, we will guide you through the process of selecting the most appropriate numerical method for various problem types, implementing them effectively, and interpreting the results accurately.

Throughout this chapter, we will navigate through ordinary differential equations (ODEs) and partial differential equations (PDEs), each presenting its unique set of challenges and opportunities. From the classic Euler's method to advanced finite element analysis, you will gain insights into a diverse array of numerical tools and their applications, ultimately equipping you with the skills to tackle a wide range of real-world problems.

The Problem

Given is an IVP of a first-order DE of the form

y′(x)=f(x,y(x)) ,y(x0)=y0 , y'(x) = f\big( x, y(x) \big)\,,\quad y(x_0) = y_0\,, y′(x)=f(x,y(x)),y(x0​)=y0​,

where f ⁣:[x0,∞)×Rdf\colon [x_0, \infty) \times \mathbb R^df:[x0​,∞)×Rd is a function and the initial condition y0∈Rdy_0\in \mathbb R^dy0​∈Rd is a given vector. Assuming that the true solution y(x)y(x)y(x) to (1) cannot be found (easily) we would like to construct a numerical approximation y^(x)\hat y(x)y^​(x) that is accurate enough, i.e., y(x)≈y^(x)y(x) \approx \hat y(x)y(x)≈y^​(x) in some sense.

Euler Method

One way of approximating the solution to (1) is to use tangent lines. From the definition of direction fields we know that any solution to an IVP must follow the flow of these tangent lines. Hence, a solution curve must have a shape similar to these lines. We use the linearization of the unknown solution y(x)y(x)y(x) of (1) at x=x0x=x_0x=x0​

L(x)=y0+f(x0,y0)(x−x0) . L(x) = y_0 + f(x_0, y_0)(x-x_0)\,. L(x)=y0​+f(x0​,y0​)(x−x0​).

The graph of this linearization is a straight line tangent to the graph of y=y(x)y=y(x)y=y(x) at the point (x0,y0)(x_0, y_0)(x0​,y0​). We now let h>0h>0h>0 be an increment of the xxx-axis. Then by replacing xxx by x1=x0+hx_1 = x_0 + hx1​=x0​+h in (2), we get

L(x1)=y0+f(x0,y0)(x0+h−x0)ory1=y0+hf(x1,y1) , L(x_1) = y_0 + f(x_0, y_0)(x_0 + h - x_0) \quad\text{or}\quad y_1 = y_0 + h f(x_1, y_1)\,, L(x1​)=y0​+f(x0​,y0​)(x0​+h−x0​)ory1​=y0​+hf(x1​,y1​),

where y1=L(x1)y_1 = L(x_1)y1​=L(x1​). The point (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1​,y1​) on the tangent line is an approximation to the true solution (x1,y(x1))(x_1, y(x_1))(x1​,y(x1​)) on the solution curve. The accuracy of the approximation L(x1)≈y(x1)L(x_1) \approx y(x_1)L(x1​)≈y(x1​) or y1≈y(x1)y_1\approx y(x_1)y1​≈y(x1​) depends heavily on the size of the increment hhh and the smoothness of yyy. Usually, the step size hhh is chosen to be reasonably small.

By identifying the new starting points as (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1​,y1​) with (x0,y0)(x_0, y_0)(x0​,y0​) in the above discussion, we obtain an approximation y2≈y(x2)y_2 \approx y(x_2)y2​≈y(x2​) corresponding to two steps of length hhh from x0x_0x0​, that is, x2=x1+h=x0+2hx_2 = x_1 + h = x_0 + 2hx2​=x1​+h=x0​+2h, and

y(x2)=y(x0+2h)=y(x1+h)≈y2=y1+hf(x1,y1) . y(x_2) = y(x_0 + 2h) = y(x_1 + h) \approx y_2 = y_1 + h f(x_1, y_1)\,. y(x2​)=y(x0​+2h)=y(x1​+h)≈y2​=y1​+hf(x1​,y1​).

Continuing in this manner, we see that y1,y2,y3,…y_1, y_2, y_3, \ldotsy1​,y2​,y3​,…, can be defined recursively by the general formula for Euler's Method

yn+1=yn+hf(xn,yn) , y_{n+1} = y_n + hf(x_n, y_n)\,, yn+1​=yn​+hf(xn​,yn​),

where xn=x0+nhx_n = x_0 + nhxn​=x0​+nh, n=0,1,2,…n=0, 1, 2, \ldotsn=0,1,2,….

Example: Approximate Nonlinear ODE

Consider the IVP y′=0.1y+0.4x2y'=0.1\sqrt{y} + 0.4x^2y′=0.1y​+0.4x2, y(−2)=1y(-2) = 1y(−2)=1. We are going to use Euler's method to obtain an approximation using first h=1.0h=1.0h=1.0 and then h=0.5h=0.5h=0.5.

using Plots
gr()

function euler(f, x0, xn, y0, n)
    h = (xn - x0)/n
  
    xs = zeros(n+1)
    ys = zeros(n+1)
  
    xs[1] = x0
    ys[1] = y0
  
    for i in 1:n
      xs[i + 1] = xs[i] + h
      ys[i + 1] = ys[i] + h * f(xs[i], ys[i])
    end
  
    return xs, ys
end

f(x, y) = 0.1*√y + 0.4*x^2

x_0 = -2
x_n = 2
y_0 = 0.5

h_1 = 1.0
h_2 = 0.5

n_1 = floor(Int, (x_n-x_0) / h_1)
n_2 = floor(Int, (x_n-x_0) / h_2)

plt = plot()
xs, ys = euler(f, x_0, x_n, y_0, n_1)
plot!(xs, ys, label="h=1.0", marker=(:circle))

xs, ys = euler(f, x_0, x_n, y_0, n_2)
plot!(xs, ys, label="h=0.5", marker=(:rect))

Euler’s method is just one of many different ways in which a solution of a DE can be approximated. Although attractive for its simplicity, Euler’s method is seldom used in serious calculations. It was introduced here simply to give you a first taste of numerical methods. We will go into greater detail in discussing numerical methods that give significantly greater accuracy.

Runge-Kutta Methods

Probably one of the more popular as well as most accurate numerical procedures used in obtaining approximate solutions to a first-order IVP y′=f(x,y)y'=f(x, y)y′=f(x,y), y(x0)=y0y(x_0) = y_0y(x0​)=y0​ is the fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK) method. As the name suggests, there are RK methods of different orders. RK methods are a class of methods which uses the information on the slope at more than one point to extrapolate the solution to future time step.

Motivation

Assume a function y(x)y(x)y(x) is k+1k+1k+1 times continuously differentiable on an open interval containing aaa and xxx. Then the Taylor polynomial can be used to write

y(x)=y(a)+y′(a)x−a1!+y′′(a)(x−a)22!+…+y(k+1)(c)(x−a)k+1(k+1)! , y(x) = y(a) + y'(a) \frac{x-a}{1!} + y''(a)\frac{(x-a)^2}{2!} + \ldots + y^{(k+1)}(c) \frac{(x-a)^{k+1}}{(k+1)!}\,, y(x)=y(a)+y′(a)1!x−a​+y′′(a)2!(x−a)2​+…+y(k+1)(c)(k+1)!(x−a)k+1​,

where ccc is some number between aaa and xxx. After replacing aaa and xxx by xnx_nxn​ and xn+1=xn+hx_{n+1} = x_n +hxn+1​=xn​+h, respectively, the formula turns into

y(xn+1)=y(xn+h)=y(xn)+hy′(xn)+h22!y′′(xn)+…+hk+1(k+1)!y(k+1)(c) , y(x_{n+1}) = y(x_n+h) = y(x_n) + hy'(x_n) + \frac{h^2}{2!}y''(x_n) + \ldots + \frac{h^{k+1}}{(k+1)!}y^{(k+1)}(c)\,, y(xn+1​)=y(xn​+h)=y(xn​)+hy′(xn​)+2!h2​y′′(xn​)+…+(k+1)!hk+1​y(k+1)(c),

where c∈(xn,xn+1)c\in (x_n, x_{n+1})c∈(xn​,xn+1​). When y(x)y(x)y(x) is a solution to y′=f(x,y)y'=f(x,y)y′=f(x,y) in the case k=1k=1k=1 and the remainder h2/2y′′(c)h^2/2y''(c)h2/2y′′(c) is close to 0, we see that the Taylor polynomial y(xn+1)=y(xn)+hy′(xn)y(x_{n+1}) = y(x_n) + hy'(x_n)y(xn+1​)=y(xn​)+hy′(xn​) of degree one agrees with the approximation formula of Eulers' method (3). Parameters of mmm-order RK methods are chosen such that they agree with a Taylor polynomial of degree mmm.

Second-Order Runge-Kutta Method

To further illustrate the motivation behind RK methods we derive a second-order RK procedure from scratch. Euler's method approximates y(xn+1)y(x_{n+1})y(xn+1​) with the derivative f(xn,yn)f(x_n, y_n)f(xn​,yn​) which we call k1k_1k1​ from now on. To get a better approximation we would like to incorporate the derivative at the halfway point between xnx_nxn​ and xn+1=xn+hx_{n+1} = x_n + hxn+1​=xn​+h labeled k2k_2k2​. The problem is we do not know the exact value of y(xn+h/2)y(x_n + h / 2)y(xn​+h/2) so we also cannot find the exact value of k2k_2k2​ at xn+h/2x_n + h/2xn​+h/2. Instead, we approximate y(xn+h/2)y(x_n + h/2)y(xn​+h/2) using a first-order RK method and use it to approximate the slope at the midpoint k2k_2k2​. The algorithm is outlined below.

  1. Estimate derivative k1=f(xn,yn)k_1 = f(x_n, y_n)k1​=f(xn​,yn​)

  2. Approximate function at midpoint y(xn+h/2)≈yn+k1h/2=y∗y(x_n + h/2) \approx y_n + k_1 h / 2 = y^*y(xn​+h/2)≈yn​+k1​h/2=y∗

  3. Estimate slope at midpoint k2=f(xn+h/2,y∗)k_2 = f(x_n + h/2, y^*)k2​=f(xn​+h/2,y∗)

  4. Final approximation y(xn+1)≈yn+1=yn+k2hy(x_{n+1}) \approx y_{n+1} = y_n + k_2 hy(xn+1​)≈yn+1​=yn​+k2​h

We can further generalize this concept by replacing the midpoint with fractional values α,β∈[0,1]\alpha,\beta\in [0,1]α,β∈[0,1]. The approximations of the derivative and the update equation are then defined as

k1=f(xn,yn)k2=f(xn+αh,yn+βhk1)yn+1=yn+h(w1k1+w2k2) \begin{aligned} k_1 &= f(x_n, y_n)\\ k_2 &= f(x_n + \alpha h, y_n + \beta h k_1)\\ y_{n+1} &= y_n + h(w_1 k_1 + w_2 k_2) \end{aligned} k1​k2​yn+1​​=f(xn​,yn​)=f(xn​+αh,yn​+βhk1​)=yn​+h(w1​k1​+w2​k2​)​

Note that instead of only using one approximation of the derivative we now update the value with an average of both approximations. The previous method is described by α=β=1/2\alpha=\beta = 1/2α=β=1/2, but other choices are possible as well. The goal is to find values w1,w2,α,βw_1, w_2, \alpha, \betaw1​,w2​,α,β such that the resulting error is low. Computing the two-dimensional Taylor series of f(x,y)f(x,y)f(x,y) to expand the k2k_2k2​ term yields

f(xn+αh,yn+βhk1)=f+fxαh+fyβhk1+…=f+fxαh+fyfβh+… . \begin{aligned} f(x_n + \alpha h, y_n + \beta h k_1) &= f + f_x \alpha h+ f_y\beta h k_1 + \ldots \\ &= f + f_x \alpha h + f_y f\beta h + \ldots\,. \end{aligned} f(xn​+αh,yn​+βhk1​)​=f+fx​αh+fy​βhk1​+…=f+fx​αh+fy​fβh+….​

After plugging this back into (5) we get

yn+1=yn+h(w1k1+w2k2)=yn+hw1k1+hw2(f+fxαh+fyfβh+…)=yn+hw1f+hw2(f+fxαh+fyfβh+…)=yn+h(w1+w2)f+h2w2fxα+h2w2fyfβ+… . \begin{aligned} y_{n+1} &= y_n + h(w_1 k_1 + w_2 k_2) \\ &= y_n + h w_1 k_1 + hw_2 (f + f_x \alpha h + f_y f\beta h + \ldots) \\ &= y_n + h w_1 f + hw_2 (f + f_x \alpha h + f_y f\beta h + \ldots) \\ &= y_n + h(w_1 + w_2)f + h^2 w_2 f_x \alpha + h^2 w_2 f_y f \beta + \ldots\,. \end{aligned} yn+1​​=yn​+h(w1​k1​+w2​k2​)=yn​+hw1​k1​+hw2​(f+fx​αh+fy​fβh+…)=yn​+hw1​f+hw2​(f+fx​αh+fy​fβh+…)=yn​+h(w1​+w2​)f+h2w2​fx​α+h2w2​fy​fβ+….​

Now we compare this expression to the Taylor polynomial of the exact solution given in (4). Note that according to the chain rule it holds that

Generalization

The slope function fff is replaced by a weighted average of slopes over the interval xn≤x≤xn+1x_n \leq x \leq x_{n+1}xn​≤x≤xn+1​. That is,

yn+1=yn+h(w1k1+w2k2+…+wmkm)=yn+h∑j=1mwjkj . y_{n+1} = y_n + h(w_1 k_1 + w_2 k_2 + \ldots + w_m k_m) = y_n + h\sum_{j=1}^m w_j k_j\,. yn+1​=yn​+h(w1​k1​+w2​k2​+…+wm​km​)=yn​+hj=1∑m​wj​kj​.

The kjk_jkj​ are defined recursively like

Example: Comparing Solvers

We compare the performance of various numerical methods on the simple harmonic oscillator problem given by

x¨+ω2x=0 . \ddot{x} + \omega^2 x = 0\,. x¨+ω2x=0.

The classical RK4 method is given by the following tableau.

01/21/21/201/210011/61/31/31/6 \begin{array}{c|cccc} 0 & & & & \\ 1/2 & 1/2 & & & \\ 1/2 & 0 & 1/2 & & \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & \\ \hline & 1/6 & 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/6 \end{array} 01/21/21​1/2001/6​1/201/3​11/3​1/6​​
using OrdinaryDiffEq, PlotlyJS

# parameters
ω = 1

# initial conditions
x₀ = [0.0]
dx₀ = [π/2]
tspan = (0.0, 2π)
ts = 0:0.05:2π

# known analytical solution
ϕ = atan((dx₀[1] / ω) / x₀[1])
A = √(x₀[1]^2 + dx₀[1]^2)
truesolution(t) = @. A * cos(ω * t - ϕ)

# define the problem
function harmonicoscillator(ddu, du, u, ω, t)
    return @. ddu = -ω^2 * u
end
prob = SecondOrderODEProblem(harmonicoscillator, dx₀, x₀, tspan, ω)

# pass to solvers
midpoint = solve(prob, Midpoint())
rk4 = solve(prob, RK4())
tsit5 = solve(prob, Tsit5())

# plot results
traces = [
    PlotlyJS.scatter(x=ts, y=truesolution(ts), name="true", mode="lines"),
    PlotlyJS.scatter(x=midpoint.t, y=last.(midpoint.u), name="midpoint", mode="lines+markers"),
    PlotlyJS.scatter(x=rk4.t, y=last.(rk4.u), name="rk4", mode="lines+markers"),
    PlotlyJS.scatter(x=tsit5.t, y=last.(tsit5.u), name="tsit5", mode="lines+markers"),
]
plt = PlotlyJS.plot(traces)

As the numerical solvers grow in complexity from top to bottom the adaptive number of steps is reduced drastically. For a full list of available solvers for ODE problems we refer to https://docs.sciml.ai/DiffEqDocs/stable/solvers/ode_solve/ in the DifferentialEquations.jl package.
